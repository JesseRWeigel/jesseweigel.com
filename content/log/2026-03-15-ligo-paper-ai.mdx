I pointed an autonomous research copilot at LIGO gravitational wave data and walked away. Three days later, it had trained two models, caught its own numerical bugs, and produced a 16-page paper ready for arXiv.

## The Experiment

Get Physics Done (GPD) is one of my autonomous research copilots — give it a research question, and it does the whole pipeline: literature review, data acquisition, model training, analysis, and paper writing. I pointed it at a question I'd been curious about: are Vision Transformers actually better than CNNs for classifying glitches in gravitational wave detector data?

GPD ran for about 72 hours. It:
- Downloaded the Gravity Spy dataset (22 glitch classes from LIGO O3)
- Trained a ViT-B/16 and a ResNet-50 with identical hyperparameters
- Ran class-level analysis across all 22 categories
- Caught a critical bug in its own numerical analysis
- Wrote and formatted a complete paper

## The Bug It Caught

This is the part I find most interesting. In its initial results, GPD reported that ViT had a 3.4x advantage over CNN on certain glitch classes. But during its self-verification step, it discovered that `np.interp` was clamping boundary values, which inflated the reported improvement. The real advantage was 1.01x — essentially a wash for those classes.

An AI found and corrected its own numerical error. That's not cherry-picked for the blog post; it's in the commit history.

## The Actual Results

The paper found something genuinely interesting: neither architecture is universally better. Performance depends heavily on the glitch class. CNNs handle certain morphologies better; ViTs excel at others. This has practical implications for LIGO's continuous wave searches.

## What This Means

I built GPD and then adapted the architecture into eight domain-specific copilots — the Get-X-Done series. Math, law, chemistry, biology, quantitative finance, engineering, and policy. The math one produced a 9-page proof improving an Alon 1997 chromatic number bound.

These aren't toys. They produce real, verifiable research output. They're also not replacements for researchers — they're tireless first-draft machines that catch their own mistakes and write clean LaTeX.
